About Amphibians

  • Amphibians have moist, smooth skin that helps them breathe through their skin.
  • They usually lay soft, jelly-like eggs in water.
  • Many amphibians, like frogs and salamanders, can live both on land and in water.
  • They often start life as aquatic larvae and later develop into adults that can live on land.
  • Amphibians are cold-blooded and depend on their environment to regulate body temperature.

Frogs

Frogs are amphibians known for their smooth, moist skin and powerful hind legs that help them jump long distances. They usually live near water, as they lay their jelly-like eggs in ponds or streams. Frogs go through a life cycle called metamorphosis, starting as aquatic tadpoles before transforming into adult frogs. They are skilled hunters, using their long, sticky tongues to catch insects and other small prey, and they play an important role in controlling insect populations.

Toads

Toads are amphibians that usually have dry, bumpy skin and shorter legs compared to frogs. They are well adapted to living on land, although they return to water to lay their jelly-like eggs. Toads move with short hops or walking steps rather than long jumps. They feed on insects, worms, and other small creatures, helping control pest populations.

Salamander

Salamanders are amphibians with slender bodies, long tails, and smooth, moist skin. They are often mistaken for lizards, but unlike reptiles, salamanders have soft, permeable skin and usually need moist environments to survive. Most salamanders lay their eggs in water, and their young often start life as aquatic larvae before developing into adults. They are skilled hunters, feeding on insects, worms, and small invertebrates.
