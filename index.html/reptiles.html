About Reptiles

  • Reptiles have dry, scaly skin that helps prevent water loss.
  • Most reptiles lay eggs with leathery shells on land.
  • Some reptiles, like lizards and snakes, are commonly found near human habitats.
  • Many reptiles bask in the sun to regulate their body temperature.
  • Reptiles use their sharp teeth or strong jaws to catch and eat prey.

Snake

Snakes are reptiles with long, legless bodies covered in scales that help them move smoothly across various surfaces. They use their forked tongue and a special organ called the Jacobsonâ€™s organ to detect smells and locate prey or sense danger. Most snakes swallow their food whole with the help of flexible jaws, and their diet includes rodents, birds, frogs, and other small animals. Some species are venomous, using their venom to hunt or protect themselves, while others kill their prey by constriction

Alligator

Alligators are large reptiles found mainly in freshwater habitats like rivers, lakes, and swamps. They have a broad, U-shaped snout, powerful tails, and tough, scaly skin that helps protect them. Alligators are carnivorous, feeding on fish, birds, mammals, and other small animals. They are excellent swimmers and can also move quickly on land for short distances. Female alligators build nests from vegetation to lay their eggs, and they guard their young after hatching.

Chameleon

Chameleons are unique reptiles known for their ability to change skin color to blend with their surroundings, regulate body temperature, or communicate. They have long, sticky tongues used to catch insects and eyes that can move independently, allowing them to look in two directions at once. Chameleons have zygodactyl feet for gripping branches and prehensile tails that help them balance in trees. Most chameleons live in warm climates, particularly in Africa and Madagascar.
